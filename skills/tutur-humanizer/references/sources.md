# Sources

Use these references when the task requires Indonesian correctness, register judgment, or terminology choices.

## Official References

- EYD V: https://ejaan.kemdikbud.go.id/
  - Use for spelling, capitalization, word writing, punctuation, and loanword spelling.
  - Treat EYD V as the default authority for official/formal Indonesian writing.

- KBBI Daring: https://kbbi.kemdikbud.go.id/
  - Use for lemma checks, meanings, usage labels, and register labels.
  - Do not assume every KBBI entry is formal/baku in every context. Check labels such as `cak` for cakapan, `kas` for kasar, `hor` for hormat, and regional labels.

- KBBI Petunjuk Pemakaian: https://kbbi.kemendikdasmen.go.id/Content/Files/Petunjuk%20Pemakaian.PDF
  - Use for understanding KBBI labels, language variety, dialect, idiolect, and register.
  - Important editorial rule: informal/cakapan words can be valid Indonesian but still unsuitable for official writing.

- Pedoman Umum Pembentukan Istilah: https://badanbahasa.kemendikdasmen.go.id/resource/doc/files/Pedoman_Umum_Pembentukan_Istilah_PBN_0.pdf
  - Use for terminology choices.
  - Prefer terms that are precise, concise, have suitable connotation, sound natural, and follow Indonesian word-formation rules.

## Practical Editorial Position

Tutur should not turn every text into rigid textbook Indonesian. The goal is register control:

- Official text should be standard, clean, and restrained.
- Public/professional text should be clear, credible, and not bureaucratic.
- Casual text may use nonstandard forms when they match the target reader.
- Slang should be intentional, limited, and socially appropriate.

## Research Notes

KBBI distinguishes language variation by use and relationship between speakers. This matters for humanization because "natural" is not the same as "formal". A WhatsApp caption, public landing page, official letter, academic abstract, and sales DM need different choices.

The biggest Indonesian AI-writing problem is often not grammar. It is register mismatch: formal words in casual contexts, translated English phrasing, vague claims, stacked clauses, and copywriting hype that says little.
